# SuperLiar Dice - Named Strategy & Pattern Library

> 12 recognizable bluffing/playing patterns for real-time opponent labeling.
> Each pattern has a Vietnamese-friendly name, behavioral signals, usage, and counters.

---

## 1. Leo Chac (Leo Chac - The Slow Build)
**EN:** The Slow Build / Sandbagging

**What it looks like:**
- Player bids conservatively, only incrementing by +1 quantity each turn
- Stays on the same face value for multiple rounds
- Avoids drawing attention; bids feel "safe" and boring
- Often holds strong dice but hides them behind modest bids

**When to use it:**
- Early game when many dice are in play (high probability covers you)
- When you actually HAVE the dice you're bidding (semi-honest play)
- To lull opponents into a false sense of security before a trap

**How to counter it:**
- Track their face choice -- if they keep bidding the same face, they likely hold it
- Force them off their comfort zone by switching faces
- Don't challenge them early; let them overcommit, then challenge when the quantity gets unreasonable

---

## 2. Bao Lua (Bao Lua - The Storm)
**EN:** The Pressure Cooker / Bid Jumping

**What it looks like:**
- Aggressive quantity jumps: instead of "four 3s" to "five 3s", jumps to "eight 3s"
- Designed to skip past other players and force an immediate Dudo/Liar call
- Often accompanied by confident body language or rapid bidding
- Creates panic -- other players must either overbid or challenge

**When to use it:**
- When you hold 3+ of a face and wilds, making the jump statistically defensible
- To eliminate a weak player who's low on dice
- When the round has been going too long and you want to force a resolution

**How to counter it:**
- Do the math: count total dice in play, estimate if the jump is plausible (1/3 rule)
- If they jump on a face YOU also hold, it might be real -- let it pass
- If you have zero of that face, consider challenging -- they may be pure bluffing

---

## 3. Ma Guong (Ma Guong - The Mirror)
**EN:** The Echo / The Mirror

**What it looks like:**
- Player copies the face value chosen by the previous bidder, only raising quantity
- Never introduces a new face value on their own
- Appears passive, but is actually gathering information
- "You bid fives? I also bid fives. More fives."

**When to use it:**
- When you genuinely hold some of the same face (piggyback on truth)
- To avoid revealing information about your own dice
- In mid-game when you want others to take the risk of switching

**How to counter it:**
- Switch faces suddenly -- a mirror player will be forced to either follow (revealing they're just copying) or break pattern
- The mirror player's information is hidden; track how often they mirror vs. lead
- If they always mirror, they're likely playing defensively with weak dice

---

## 4. Doi Mat (Doi Mat - The Switch / Face Flip)
**EN:** The Switch / The Pivot

**What it looks like:**
- Player suddenly changes from one face value to a completely different one
- Example: bidding 4s all round, then suddenly claims "six 2s"
- Often done mid-round to disrupt tracking and confuse opponents
- Can signal either a genuine strong hand in the new face, or a desperation bluff

**When to use it:**
- When your current face is getting too high in quantity and you need an escape route
- To throw off opponents who were counting your previous face
- When you actually hold a pocket of the new face (semi-bluff switch)

**How to counter it:**
- A switch usually means the player was NOT strong on the original face
- Check: do YOU have any of their new face? If you have many, they're likely bluffing
- The switch often happens right before a bluff -- be ready to challenge

---

## 5. Bay Gia (Bay Gia - The Trap / Slow Play)
**EN:** The Trap / Slow Play / Sandbagging

**What it looks like:**
- Player holds an extremely strong hand (4-5 of the same face + wilds)
- Bids very low and passive, waiting for others to raise the quantity
- Lets opponents build confidence and overbid
- Springs the trap by letting someone else get challenged, or by making a seemingly impossible "exact" call (Calza)

**When to use it:**
- When you hold 4+ of a single face -- let others do the bidding for you
- When another player is being aggressive; let them hang themselves
- In heads-up (1v1) endgame situations

**How to counter it:**
- Watch for a player who NEVER initiates a face but always follows
- If someone seems too relaxed while bids climb, they might be trapping
- Switch faces away from the "obvious" one -- force them to reveal their hand

---

## 6. Lua Lanh (Lua Lanh - The Cold Bluff)
**EN:** The Stone-Cold Bluff / Pure Bluff

**What it looks like:**
- Player bids a face they have ZERO of, with complete confidence
- Relies entirely on psychology -- no statistical backup
- Often uses strong eye contact, quick decisive bids, casual tone
- The "poker face" play: selling a lie as absolute truth

**When to use it:**
- Sparingly! Maximum once per game session to maintain credibility
- When you're short-stacked (1-2 dice) and have nothing to lose
- Against timid players who rarely challenge

**How to counter it:**
- Track frequency: if a player bids confidently every round, some of those must be bluffs
- Count YOUR dice of that face -- if you hold 3+ of it, their claim gets less plausible
- The "too confident" tell: overly smooth delivery can indicate a rehearsed bluff

---

## 7. Neo Don (Neo Don - The Anchor)
**EN:** The Anchor / The Frame Setter

**What it looks like:**
- First player in the round sets an unusually high or strategic opening bid
- This "anchors" all subsequent bids around their chosen face and quantity
- Controls the narrative of the entire round
- Example: opening with "four 6s" when only 15 dice are in play

**When to use it:**
- When you're first to bid and hold 2-3 of a face with wilds
- To steer the round toward a face where you're strong
- To psychologically intimidate: a high opener suggests confidence

**How to counter it:**
- Don't be anchored! The opener's bid is just one data point
- Switch to a different face early to break the anchor's power
- If you're second and the anchor seems too high, challenge immediately -- early anchors can be pure bluffs

---

## 8. Doi Khi (Doi Khi - The Chameleon)
**EN:** The Unpredictable / Mixed Strategy

**What it looks like:**
- Player alternates between aggressive and conservative play with no clear pattern
- Sometimes bluffs hard, sometimes plays honest, sometimes mirrors
- Impossible to "read" because they have no consistent tell
- Uses randomness as a weapon

**When to use it:**
- This is the ADVANCED strategy: mix all other patterns deliberately
- Prevents opponents from building a model of your behavior
- Especially effective in longer sessions where pattern recognition kicks in

**How to counter it:**
- Focus on math over psychology -- the chameleon beats readers, not calculators
- Don't try to read them; instead, play your own strong probability game
- Force them into corners where the math doesn't support any strategy

---

## 9. Goi Han (Goi Han - The Squeeze)
**EN:** The Squeeze / The Cornering

**What it looks like:**
- Player deliberately positions bids so the NEXT player (target) faces an impossible choice
- Either the target must overbid into dangerous territory or challenge
- Works best in 3+ player games by manipulating turn order
- "I'll bid just high enough that HE has to deal with it, not me"

**When to use it:**
- When a specific opponent is low on dice (1-2 remaining)
- When you can predict the target will be afraid to challenge
- To eliminate a dangerous player by forcing them into a lose-lose

**How to counter it:**
- Recognize when you're being squeezed -- don't panic-bid
- Sometimes the correct play when squeezed is to challenge, even if risky
- If squeezed, switch faces rather than raising quantity on their terms

---

## 10. Doc Gia (Doc Gia - The Tell Reader)
**EN:** The Profiler / The Cold Reader

**What it looks like:**
- Player watches opponents' micro-behaviors: hesitation speed, eye movement, breathing
- Makes decisions based on reads rather than probability
- Waits slightly longer before bidding (observing others)
- May ask questions or make comments to provoke reactions

**When to use it:**
- Against opponents with visible tells (new players especially)
- In live/voice games where body language is visible
- When probability is 50/50 and you need a tiebreaker

**Behavioral signals to watch for:**
- Quick peek at dice after hearing a bid = checking if they have that face
- Hesitation before bidding = uncertainty about their claim
- Overly casual tone = often hiding a weak hand
- Fidgeting or touching face = stress from bluffing
- Immediate confident bid = likely honest (or very experienced bluffer)

**How to counter it:**
- Maintain consistent behavior regardless of hand strength
- Don't look at your dice when others bid
- Randomize your bid timing -- don't always bid fast when honest

---

## 11. Rut Lui (Rut Lui - The Retreat)
**EN:** The Strategic Retreat / The Sacrifice

**What it looks like:**
- Player intentionally makes a bid they expect to lose
- Sacrifices one die to gain information about opponents' dice
- Also used to avoid a worse outcome (losing the challenge would reveal more)
- "I'll take the hit now to set up a better position next round"

**When to use it:**
- When you have plenty of dice and can afford to lose one
- To test whether an opponent is bluffing (call Dudo on a marginal bid)
- To establish a reputation as someone who challenges often (deterrent effect)

**How to counter it:**
- If someone frequently sacrifices, they're playing the long game
- Don't be intimidated by frequent challengers -- they're paying a cost too
- Use their aggression against them: make solid bids they can't profitably challenge

---

## 12. Song Sat (Song Sat - The Double Game)
**EN:** The Semi-Bluff / The Hedge

**What it looks like:**
- Player bids a face where they hold SOME (1-2) but not enough to justify the bid alone
- The bid is a stretch, but not a pure lie -- wilds and other players' dice might cover it
- Sits between honest play and full bluff
- "I have two 4s, and I'll bid five 4s hoping the table has the rest"

**When to use it:**
- Most of the time! This is the most common and safest bluffing strategy
- When you hold 1-2 of a face and the probability suggests the total is reasonable
- As a foundation bid that others will build on

**How to counter it:**
- Hard to counter because it's the "correct" play most of the time
- Challenge only when the quantity exceeds expected probability significantly
- If you have ZERO of their face, the math shifts -- their semi-bluff becomes riskier

## Quick Reference: Pattern Detection Cheat Sheet

| Signal | Likely Pattern | Confidence |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| Same face, +1 each turn | Leo Chac (Slow Build) | High |
| Big quantity jump | Bao Lua (Storm) | High |
| Always copies previous face | Ma Guong (Mirror) | Medium |
| Sudden face change | Doi Mat (Switch) | High |
| Passive with strong hand | Bay Gia (Trap) | Medium |
| Confident bid on unlikely face | Lua Lanh (Cold Bluff) | Medium |
| High opening bid | Neo Don (Anchor) | High |
| No consistent pattern | Doi Khi (Chameleon) | Low |
| Targets specific player | Goi Han (Squeeze) | High |
| Watches before bidding | Doc Gia (Reader) | Medium |
| Accepts losing a die calmly | Rut Lui (Retreat) | Medium |
| Partial truth, stretched bid | Song Sat (Semi-Bluff) | Low (hard to spot) |

## AI Coach Detection Logic (Implementation Notes)

For the SuperLiar training game, the AI can detect these patterns by tracking:

1. **Bid delta** (quantity change per turn) -- detects Slow Build vs Storm
2. **Face consistency** (same face across turns) -- detects Mirror, Anchor
3. **Face switches** (frequency of changing face value) -- detects Switch, Chameleon
4. **Bid vs probability** (bid quantity / expected quantity ratio) -- detects Cold Bluff, Semi-Bluff
5. **Target analysis** (who is forced to respond after their bid) -- detects Squeeze
6. **Challenge frequency** (how often they call Dudo) -- detects Retreat, Reader
7. **Opening position** (behavior when first to bid) -- detects Anchor
8. **Hand correlation** (bid face matches known dice?) -- detects Trap, Honest play

### Labeling Thresholds (suggested)
- If bid_delta <= 1 for 3+ consecutive turns: tag `leo_chac`
- If bid_delta >= 3 in a single turn: tag `bao_lua`
- If face matches previous bidder 3+ times in a row: tag `ma_guong`
- If face changes after 2+ consistent turns: tag `doi_mat`
- If bid_quantity / expected_quantity > 1.5 AND player holds 0 of face: tag `lua_lanh`
- If bid_quantity / expected_quantity > 1.2 AND player holds 1-2 of face: tag `song_sat`
- If opening bid > 40% of expected max: tag `neo_don`
